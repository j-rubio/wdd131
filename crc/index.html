Jewish Law & Daily Rituals

Written vs. Oral Law:

The Written Law (Torah Shebiktav) refers to the Five Books of Moses. The Oral Law (Torah Sheba’al Peh) includes the interpretations and details given to Moses at Sinai (later recorded in the Mishnah and Talmud) that explain how to fulfill the Written Law’s commandments.

Meal with Bread:

The first requirement is to wash your hands (Netilat Yadayim) before reciting the blessing of Hamotzi.

Morning Hand Washing Method:

Use a vessel to pour water over your hands alternating between right and left three times (Right, Left, Right, Left, Right, Left) up to the wrist.

First Action Upon Waking:

Recite the Modeh Ani prayer to thank Hashem for returning your soul.

How to Wash (Morning):

(Duplicate of #3) Alternate three times on each hand using a cup.

Why Wash in the Morning:

Two main reasons: 1) To remove the "unclean spirit" (ruach ra) that rests on the hands during sleep, and 2) To purify oneself for the service of Hashem, similar to a Priest in the Temple.

Meat to Dairy / Dairy to Meat:

- **Meat to Dairy**: Wait **6 hours**. - **Dairy to Meat**: No waiting is required for most dairy (just rinse the mouth and wash hands), but for **hard/aged cheese**, one must wait **6 hours**.

Parve:

Refers to neutral foods that are neither meat nor dairy (e.g., fruits, vegetables, fish, eggs) and can be eaten with either.

Mezuzah:

- **What it is**: A parchment scroll attached to doorposts. - **Inside**: Hand-written verses from the Torah (the first two paragraphs of the *Shema*). - **Purpose**: To serve as a constant reminder of Hashem’s presence, our commitment to His commandments, and to distinguish a Jewish home.

Prayer Requirements:

Men are required to pray three times daily (Shacharit, Mincha, Ma’ariv). Women are required to pray at least once daily (though many opinions require twice), focusing on seeking Divine mercy.

Holidays & Fast Days

Shavuot:

Celebrates the Giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai.

Sukkot::

Commemorates the Clouds of Glory (or physical huts) that protected the Israelites in the desert.

Pesach (Passover):

Celebrates the Exodus from Egypt and our liberation from slavery.

Purim:

Celebrates the salvation of the Jewish people from Haman’s plot in ancient Persia.

Chanukah:

Celebrates the miracle of the oil and the Maccabean victory over the Greeks to rededicate the Temple.

Tisha B'Av:

A major fast day mourning the destruction of both Holy Temples in Jerusalem.

17th of Tammuz:

A minor fast day marking when the walls of Jerusalem were breached.

Yom Kippur:

The Day of Atonement, the holiest day for repentance and seeking forgiveness.

Rosh Hashanah:

The Jewish New Year and Day of Judgment.

Number of Fast Days:

There are 6 communal fast days (Yom Kippur, Tisha B'Av, 17th of Tammuz, Fast of Gedaliah, 10th of Tevet, and Fast of Esther).

Major Fast Days:

Yom Kippur and Tisha B’Av (25-hour fasts).

Minor Fast Days:

17th of Tammuz, Fast of Gedaliah, 10th of Tevet, and Fast of Esther (daylight fasts).

Fast Times:

- **Major**: Begin at **sunset** and end the following night at **nightfall**. - **Minor**: Begin at **dawn** (*Alot Hashachar*) and end at **nightfall**.

Shabbat & Kiddush

Candle Lighting Process:

Light the candles, then cover your eyes and recite the blessing. Some have the custom to wave hands toward themselves three times before covering eyes.

Minimal Candles:

Two candles (representing Shamôr—"observe" and Zachôr—"remember") [cRc].

Blessing:

Baruch atah... asher kidshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'hadlik ner shel Shabbat.

Guest Rule:

A guest is generally covered by the host’s lighting, but many have the custom to light their own or contribute a small amount to the host’s candles.

Hospital Lighting:

One should use electric lights (with a blessing) if fire is not permitted for safety reasons.

Mitzvah Without Blessing:

Yes, you still fulfilled the obligation (the blessing is a Rabbinic requirement, but the act is the mitzvah itself) [cRc].

Eating/Drinking Before Kiddush:

Once Shabbat begins, one may not eat or drink (even water) until making/hearing Kiddush.

Kiddush Cup Size

Must hold at least a revi’it (approx. 3.0 to 4.4 fluid ounces, depending on custom).

Leftover Wine:

If someone drank directly from the cup, the wine is "pagum" (blemished). It should be fixed by adding a little fresh wine/juice before being used for Kiddush again.

Stam Yeinam:

Wine handled or produced by non-Jews, which is forbidden to drink.

Drinking Requirement:

One must drink at least a melo lugmav (a cheekful), roughly half of a revi’it. If not drunk, the obligation is generally not fulfilled.

Listeners' Drinking:

Only the person reciting Kiddush must drink, but it is a mitzvah for listeners to taste some as well.

Sit or Stand:

Customs vary; Ashkenazim often stand for the first part and sit for the blessing over wine, while others stand for the entire Kiddush.

Speaking After Kiddush:

If one spoke before drinking, they must repeat the blessing on the wine, but not the entire Kiddush text.

Repeating for Others:

Yes, even if you already fulfilled your obligation, you may recite Kiddush for someone who hasn't heard it yet (Arvut).

Response to Hashem's Name:

Say "Baruch Hu u'varuch Shemo" (Blessed is He and blessed is His name).

Kiddush Without Wine:

One may make Kiddush over Challah (bread) by substituting the wine blessing with Hamotzi.

Shabbat Meals & Havdalah

Loaves of Bread:

Two whole loaves (Lechem Mishneh) are required at each of the three Shabbat meals [cRc].

Amount to Eat:

At least a kezayit (the volume of an olive, approx. 1 oz) within a specific timeframe.

No Whole Loaves:

If whole loaves aren't available, use what you have, but ideally, you should seek whole ones to fulfill Lechem Mishneh.

Challah Placement:

The two loaves are placed side-by-side; for the Friday night meal, the bottom one is cut first in some customs.

Why Covered:

To avoid "shaming" the bread, which usually has priority for blessings, while we prioritize the wine for Kiddush.

Havdalah:

The ceremony performed on Saturday night to mark the separation between the holiness of Shabbat and the mundane week.

Eating Before Havdalah:

One may not eat or drink after sunset Saturday until Havdalah (except water).

Havdalah Order:

Wine (Gefen), Spices (Besamim), Candle (Ner), and the Separation blessing (Hamavdil).

Obligation:

Both men and women are obligated in Havdalah.

Melaveh Malkah:

The "Escorting of the Queen" meal eaten on Saturday night after Shabbat ends to transition into the week [cRc].

What to Eat:

Ideally a warm meal with bread, but at minimum, some fruit or snacks to honor the occasion.
