Jewish Law & Daily Rituals

Written vs. Oral Law:

The Written Law(Torah Shebiktav) refers to the Five Books of Moses.
The Oral Law(Torah Sheba’al Peh) includes the interpretations and details given to Moses at Sinai (later recorded in the Mishnah and Talmud) that explain how to fulfill the Written Law’s commandments.

Meal with Bread:

The first requirement is to wash your hands (Netilat Yadayim) before reciting the blessing of Hamotzi.

Morning Hand Washing Method:

Use a vessel to pour water over your hands alternating between right and left three times (Right, Left, Right, Left, Right, Left) up to the wrist.

First Action Upon Waking:

Recite the Modeh Ani prayer to thank Hashem for returning your soul.

How to Wash (Morning):

(Duplicate of #3) Alternate three times on each hand using a cup.

Why Wash in the Morning:

Two main reasons:
1. To remove the "unclean spirit" (ruach ra) that rests on the hands during sleep, and
2. To purify oneself for the service of Hashem, similar to a Priest in the Temple.

Meat to Dairy / Dairy to Meat:

- Meat to Dairy: Wait  6 hours.
- Dairy to Meat: No waiting is required for most dairy (just rinse the mouth and wash hands), but for  hard/aged cheese, one must wait 6 hours.

Parve:

Refers to neutral foods that are neither meat nor dairy (e.g., fruits, vegetables, fish, eggs) and can be eaten with either.

Mezuzah:

What it is: A parchment scroll attached to doorposts.
What's inside: Hand-written verses from the Torah (the first two paragraphs of the Shema).
Purpose: To serve as a constant reminder of Hashem’s presence, our commitment to His commandments, and to distinguish a Jewish home.

Prayer Requirements:

Men are required to pray three times daily (Shacharit, Mincha, Ma’ariv).
Women are required to pray at least once daily (though many opinions require twice), focusing on seeking Divine mercy.

Holidays & Fast Days

Shavuot:

What: Celebrates the Giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai.
When: Shavuot is celebrated on the 6th of Sivan in the Hebrew calendar, occurring seven weeks after Passover, typically in late May or early June on the Gregorian calendar, marking the giving of the Torah at Sinai and the wheat harvest, observed for one day in Israel and two days in the diaspora with traditions like all-night Torah study and eating dairy foods.

Sukkot::

What: Commemorates the Clouds of Glory (or physical huts) that protected the Israelites in the desert.
When: Sukkot is celebrated for seven days, beginning on the 15th of Tishrei, usually in September or October, immediately following Yom Kippur, commemorating the fall harvest and the Jews' wandering in the desert.

Pesach (Passover):

What: Celebrates the Exodus from Egypt and our liberation from slavery.
When: Pesach (Passover) is celebrated in the spring, starting on the 15th of Nisan and lasting seven days in Israel (eight days outside Israel), with observances including Seder meals and abstaining from leavened foods.

Purim:

What: Celebrates the salvation of the Jewish people from Haman’s plot in ancient Persia, as described in the Book of Esther.
When: Celebrated annually on the 14th of Adar or (Adar II in leap years), typically falling in February or March on the Gregorian calendar. In Jerusalem and other historically walled cities, it's celebrated on the 15th of Adar, a day known as Shushan Purim, because the victory there lasted an extra day.

Chanukah:

What: Celebrates the miracle of the oil burning for eight days and the Maccabean victory over the Greeks to rededicate the Temple.
When: Hanukkah, the Festival of Lights, is an eight-day Jewish holiday that starts on the 25th of Kislev, typically falling between late November and late December on the Gregorian calendar. The dates shift annually because the Jewish calendar is lunar, but it always involves lighting a menorah (or hanukkiah) for eight nights, reciting blessings, eating fried foods, and playing games like dreidel.

Tisha B'Av:

What: Commemorates the destruction of the First & Second Temples, the fall of Betar, and the expulsion of Jews from Spain.
When: Celebrated on the 9th of Av, which typically falls in late July or August, beginning at sunset on the 8th and ending at sundown on the 9th. It's the most solemn day in the Jewish calendar, commemorating the destruction of both Temples in Jerusalem and other tragedies, marked by a 25-hour fast and intense mourning practices, concluding the three-week period of increasing lament.

17th of Tammuz:

What: Commemorates with a minor fast day marking when the walls of Jerusalem were breached.
When: Celebrated on the 17th of Tammuz, Shivah Asar B'Tammuz is a Jewish fast day marking the start of the Three Weeks of mourning, commemorating Jerusalem's walls being breached by Romans, falling on the 17th day of the Hebrew month of Tammuz, which varies yearly but falls around early July on the Gregorian calendar, such as July 2, 2026. It's a minor fast with specific customs, leading to Tisha B'Av, the Temple's destruction.

Yom Kippur:

What: The Day of Atonement, the holiest day for repentance and seeking forgiveness.
When: Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement, is celebrated on the 10th of Tishrei, falling in late September or early October on the Gregorian calendar, marking the culmination of the Ten Days of Repentance after Rosh Hashanah. For example, in 2025, it began the evening of October 1st, and in 2026, it starts the evening of September 20th. It's a solemn day of fasting, prayer, and reflection, observed from sunset to nightfall the following day.

Rosh Hashanah:

What: The Jewish New Year and Day of Judgment.
When: Celebrated on the two first days of Tishrei. Rosh Hashanah, the Jewish New Year, begins at sundown on Friday, September 11, 2026, and concludes at nightfall on Sunday, September 13, 2026, marking the start of the Hebrew Year 5787. It's a two-day holiday celebrated with special prayers, sounding the shofar, and symbolic foods like apples dipped in honey for a sweet new year, beginning a period of reflection leading to Yom Kippur. Meaning: "Head of the Year," celebrating the creation of the world and the start of the High Holy Days.
Traditions: Blowing the shofar (ram's horn), festive meals with sweet foods (apples, honey), and introspection.
Greeting: "Shana Tova" (a good year).

Number of Fast Days:

There are 6 communal fast days (Yom Kippur, Tisha B'Av, 17th of Tammuz, Fast of Gedaliah, 10th of Tevet, and Fast of Esther).

Major Fast Days:

Yom Kippur and Tisha B’Av (25-hour fasts).

Minor Fast Days:

17th of Tammuz, Fast of Gedaliah, 10th of Tevet, and Fast of Esther (daylight fasts).

Fast Times:

Major: Begin at sunset and end the following night at nightfall.
Minor: Begin at dawn and end at nightfall.

Shabbat & Kiddush

Candle Lighting Process:

Light the candles, then cover your eyes and recite the blessing. Some have the custom to wave hands toward themselves three times before covering eyes.

Minimal Candles:

Two candles (representing Shamôr—"observe" and Zachôr—"remember") [cRc].

Blessing:

Baruch atah... asher kidshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'hadlik ner shel Shabbat.

Guest Rule:

A guest is generally covered by the host’s lighting, but many have the custom to light their own or contribute a small amount to the host’s candles.

Hospital Lighting:

One should use electric lights (with a blessing) if fire is not permitted for safety reasons.

Mitzvah Without Blessing:

Yes, you still fulfilled the obligation (the blessing is a Rabbinic requirement, but the act is the mitzvah itself) [cRc].

Eating/Drinking Before Kiddush:

Once Shabbat begins, one may not eat or drink (even water) until making/hearing Kiddush.

Kiddush Cup Size

Must hold at least a revi’it (approx. 3.0 to 4.4 fluid ounces, depending on custom).

Leftover Wine:

If someone drank directly from the cup, the wine is "pagum" (blemished). It should be fixed by adding a little fresh wine/juice before being used for Kiddush again.

Stam Yeinam:

Wine handled or produced by non-Jews, which is forbidden to drink.

Drinking Requirement:

One must drink at least a melo lugmav (a cheekful), roughly half of a revi’it. If not drunk, the obligation is generally not fulfilled.

Listeners' Drinking:

Only the person reciting Kiddush must drink, but it is a mitzvah for listeners to taste some as well.

Sit or Stand:

Customs vary; Ashkenazim often stand for the first part and sit for the blessing over wine, while others stand for the entire Kiddush.

Speaking After Kiddush:

If one spoke before drinking, they must repeat the blessing on the wine, but not the entire Kiddush text.

Repeating for Others:

Yes, even if you already fulfilled your obligation, you may recite Kiddush for someone who hasn't heard it yet (Arvut).

Response to Hashem's Name:

Say "Baruch Hu u'varuch Shemo" (Blessed is He and blessed is His name).

Kiddush Without Wine:

One may make Kiddush over Challah (bread) by substituting the wine blessing with Hamotzi.

Shabbat Meals & Havdalah

Loaves of Bread:

Two whole loaves (Lechem Mishneh) are required at each of the three Shabbat meals [cRc].

Amount to Eat:

At least a kezayit (the volume of an olive, approx. 1 oz) within a specific timeframe.

No Whole Loaves:

If whole loaves aren't available, use what you have, but ideally, you should seek whole ones to fulfill Lechem Mishneh.

Challah Placement:

The two loaves are placed side-by-side; for the Friday night meal, the bottom one is cut first in some customs.

Why Covered:

To avoid "shaming" the bread, which usually has priority for blessings, while we prioritize the wine for Kiddush.

Havdalah:

The ceremony performed on Saturday night to mark the separation between the holiness of Shabbat and the mundane week.

Eating Before Havdalah:

One may not eat or drink after sunset Saturday until Havdalah (except water).

Havdalah Order:

Wine (Gefen), Spices (Besamim), Candle (Ner), and the Separation blessing (Hamavdil).

Obligation:

Both men and women are obligated in Havdalah.

Melaveh Malkah:

The "Escorting of the Queen" meal eaten on Saturday night after Shabbat ends to transition into the week [cRc].

What to Eat:

Ideally a warm meal with bread, but at minimum, some fruit or snacks to honor the occasion.
